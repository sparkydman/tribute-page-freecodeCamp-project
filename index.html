Prof. Chinua Achebe

A man who defended African culture.

Chinua Achebe at the scenic academic haven of the University of Nigeria, Nsukka. On the 27th day of June 1981.

Time line of Prof. Chinua Achebe:

  • 1930: Born to the family Isaiah Okafo Achebe and Janet Anaenechi Iloegbunam in Ogidi southeast part of Nigeria currently Anambra State. He has five other siblings: Frank Okwuofu, John Chukwuemeka Ifeanyichukwu, Zinobia Uzoma, Augustine Ndubisi, and Grace Nwanneka.

  • 1936: Achebe entered St Philips' Central School. Despite his protests, he spent a week in the religious class for young children, but was quickly moved to a higher class when the school's chaplain took note of his intelligence.

  • 1948: Achebe was admitted as a Major Scholar in University College (now University of Ibadan) the university's first intake and given a bursary to study medicine. He abandoned the study of medicine and changed to English, history, and theology in order to defend Africa culture by becoming a writer.

  • 1951-1953: He served as the Herald's editor during the 1951–52 school year. Achebe wrote his first short story, "In a Village Church" and other short stories he wrote (including "The Old Order in Conflict with the New" and "Dead Men's Path") during his stay in Ibadan.

  • 1953: Achebe took up teaching job at Oba as an English teacher.

  • 1954: Achebe started working with Nigerian Broadcasting Service (NBS).

  • 1958: He published his first novel things fall apart. In the book Okonkwo struggles with the legacy of his father – a shiftless debtor fond of playing the flute – as well as the complications and contradictions that arise when white missionaries arrive in his village of Umuofia.

  • 1961: He got married to his beloved wife Christiana Chinwe Achebe, their marrage is blessed with three kids. Two boys and a girl.

  • 1960: Published no longer at ease, about a civil servant who is embroiled in the corruption of Lagos.

  • 1964: published Arrow of God, it explores the intersections of Igbo tradition and European Christianity.

  • 1966: He published A Man of the People. A bleak satire set in an unnamed African state which has just attained independence, the novel follows a teacher named Odili Samalu from the village of Anata who opposes a corrupt Minister of Culture named Nanga for his Parliament seat.

  • 1987: Achebe released his fifth novel, Anthills of the Savannah, about a military coup in the fictional West African nation of Kangan.

  • 2012: Achebe's publishers, Penguin Books, released There Was a Country: A Personal History of Biafra.

  • 2013: Fondly called the "father of African literature", Achebe died after a short illness on 21 March 2013 in Boston, United States.
